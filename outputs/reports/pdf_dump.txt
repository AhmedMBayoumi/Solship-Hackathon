ENERGY AI HACKATHON 2026
Participant Problem Statement
Read this document in full before writing any code. It defines the problem, the dataset, the
evaluation rules, and what you must present.
1 Problem Formulation
You are given a residential site in Italy connected to the utility grid, a rooftop solar PV system, and a
lithium battery. The site electrical load must be fully served at every 15-minute interval without
exception.
At each timestep, energy can come from the grid (purchased at a time-varying buy price), from the
PV system when generating, or from the battery when discharging. If there is a surplus after the
load is served, the excess may charge the battery, be exported to the grid at the prevailing sell
price, or both.
Your challenge is to build two things: a load forecasting model, and a rolling-horizon battery
dispatch controller that reduces the site electricity bill compared to the defined baselines.
Power Sign Convention
Use these conventions throughout your code, equations, and presentation:
Variable Sign Meaning
P_battery Negative (< 0) Battery charging
P_battery Positive (> 0) Battery discharging
P_grid Positive (> 0) Importing from grid
P_grid Negative (< 0) Exporting to grid

4 Challenge Structure
The challenge has one required core task and one optional extension.
CORE TASK — Required
1. Train a load forecasting model on the 2024 data.
2. Apply it to generate a 2025 load forecast.
3. Build a rolling-horizon battery dispatch controller using your forecast (see Section 7).
4. Compare your controller’s 2025 electricity bill against both baselines (Section 5).
5. Report RMSE, MAE, and NRMSE on the 2025 test set.
6. Oracle gap: re-run your controller with the actual 2025 load values as input (perfect oracle).
Report oracle savings, forecast-based savings, and the gap in € and %.
EXTENSION 1 — What is the optimal forecast horizon?
Run the full 2025 optimization for at least three different horizon lengths H (e.g., 4, 24, and 96
timesteps — 1 hour, 6 hours, and 24 hours). Report total savings and computation time for
each.
What is the minimum horizon needed to capture most of the savings benefit? Is there a point of
diminishing returns? Justify your chosen H.
Report: savings vs. horizon table with your recommended H and rationale.
5 Baselines
Report savings against both baselines. Both must appear in your final results table.
Baseline A — Historical Operation
Compute the 2025 electricity bill using the p_battery_kw column from the dataset. This is exactly
what the existing on-site controller did. Your controller must beat this — if it does not, the current
system outperforms your solution.
Baseline B — Zero-Intelligence
PV powers the load first at every timestep.
Surplus (PV > load): exported to the grid at the sell price. Battery not used.
Deficit (load > PV): bought from the grid at the buy price. Battery not used.
Battery stays at its initial SoC throughout. No charging or discharging ever occurs.
Baseline B shows the value of having a controllable battery at all. Baseline A shows the value of a
smarter controller versus the existing system.

7 Battery Dispatch Controller
Rolling-Horizon Requirement
Your controller must operate causally: at each decision step it may only use information available
up to that moment. It looks ahead over a finite forecast horizon H, solves for the optimal dispatch
over that window, executes only the first decision, then advances one step and repeats.

Energy AI Hackathon 2026 — Participant Problem Statement v4.0
A one-shot batch optimization over the full 2025 year — where the optimizer simultaneously
accesses all future load, PV, and price data — is not acceptable. It does not reflect how a real
controller operates and produces artificially inflated savings figures.
This type of controller is commonly referred to as Model Predictive Control (MPC). Reinforcement
Learning (RL) is an alternative framework, though significantly more complex to implement well
within the hackathon timeframe.
Objective Function
At each horizon window, minimise the total electricity bill:
Bill = Σ [ P_grid(t) × buy_price(t) × 🟩(P_grid > 0) − |P_grid(t)| × sell_price(t) × 🟩(P_grid < 0) ] × Δt
The first term is the cost of grid imports; the second is revenue from exports. Each applies only when
P_grid has the corresponding sign.
Hard Constraints
• Grid power: −6 kW ≤ P_grid(t) ≤ 6 kW
• Battery power: −8 kW ≤ P_battery(t) ≤ 8 kW
• State of charge: 0 ≤ SoC(t) ≤ 1
Choice of Optimization Method
You are free to choose how you solve the optimization within each horizon window. Common
approaches include rule-based heuristics, linear programming (PuLP, CVXPY, SciPy), and mixed-
integer programming. You will be judged on results and on the clarity of your reasoning for your
method choice.
Required Results
• 2025 electricity bill under Baseline A (historical) and Baseline B (zero-intelligence)
• 2025 electricity bill under your rolling-horizon controller
• Absolute savings (€) and percentage savings vs. each baseline
• Oracle gap: re-run the optimizer with actual 2025 load as input (perfect oracle). Report oracle
savings, forecast-based savings, and the gap in € and %
• Dispatch plot for Week 3 of March 2025: load, PV, P_battery, P_grid, and SoC
The March Week 3 dispatch plot is mandatory. Judges use it to verify that your controller is
behaving physically correctly and that your reported savings are consistent with the dispatch
decisions shown.
